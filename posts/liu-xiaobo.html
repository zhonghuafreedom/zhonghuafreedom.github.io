劉曉波與《零八憲章》：未死的良知 Liu Xiaobo and Charter 08: A Conscience That Refused to Die

2008年12月10日，世界人權宣言60週年當天，由劉曉波等人起草的《零八憲章》正式公布，首批簽署人達303位，後續簽名超過一萬。憲章呼籲修憲、三權分立、司法獨立、聯邦共和——這份溫和卻鋒利的政治綱領，將劉曉波推向他人生最後的牢獄。

《零八憲章》的核心主張

憲章提出19條改革主張，包括保障人權、結束一黨專政、建立聯邦共和、實現轉型正義。它並非革命宣言，而是一份知識分子集體簽名的請願書，直接對話於中共的「合法性」。

從拘押到11年重刑

2008年12月8日，憲章公布前夕，劉曉波在北京家中被警方帶走。2009年12月25日，北京市第一中級人民法院以「煽動顛覆國家政權罪」判處他有期徒刑11年——當天是聖誕節，明顯有意避開國際關注。他在法庭上的最後陳述題為《我沒有敵人》：

「我期待我的祖國是一片可以自由表達的土地，在這裡，所有國民的發言都會得到同樣善意的對待；……我希望我會是中國綿綿不絕的文字獄的最後一個受害者。」

諾貝爾和平獎與空椅子

2010年12月10日，奧斯陸頒獎典禮上一張空椅子代表了缺席的諾貝爾和平獎得主劉曉波，他正在遼寧錦州監獄服刑。中國官方憤怒抗議並動員多國抵制。然而，那把空椅子已成為21世紀人權史上最沉重的影像之一。

病逝獄中與妻子劉霞的軟禁

2017年6月，劉曉波被確診肝癌晚期，當局拒絕其出國治療的請求。7月13日，他在瀋陽中國醫科大學附屬第一醫院去世，骨灰被當局強制海葬，家屬未被允許保留。妻子劉霞長期遭非法軟禁，直至2018年才獲准赴德國治療。

結語：他的話比監獄更長久

劉曉波是1989年六四廣場上勸學生撤離以避免流血的「四君子」之一，是2008年呼籲非暴力轉型的請願者，也是中華人民共和國史上唯一在獄中辭世的諾貝爾和平獎得主。當局以為時間會讓他被遺忘，但「我沒有敵人」這句話，正悄悄成為新一代中國人良心的座右銘。

On December 10, 2008—the 60th anniversary of the Universal Declaration of Human Rights—Charter 08, co-authored by Liu Xiaobo and others, was formally released with 303 initial signatories. Later signatures grew to more than ten thousand. The Charter called for constitutional reform, separation of powers, judicial independence, and a federal republic. This moderate yet incisive political manifesto sent Liu to his final imprisonment.

The Core Demands of Charter 08

The Charter set forth 19 reform proposals, including the protection of human rights, an end to one-party rule, the establishment of a federal republic, and the pursuit of transitional justice. It was not a revolutionary manifesto but a collective petition by intellectuals—directly challenging the "legitimacy" of the Chinese Communist Party.

From Detention to an 11-Year Sentence

On December 8, 2008, two days before the Charter was published, Liu was taken from his home in Beijing. On December 25, 2009—Christmas Day, deliberately chosen to deflect international attention—Beijing's First Intermediate People's Court sentenced him to 11 years in prison for "inciting subversion of state power." His final court statement, "I Have No Enemies," reads in part:

"I look forward to my country being a land of free expression, where every citizen's voice will be treated with the same goodwill; ... I hope I will be the last victim of China's endless literary inquisitions."

The Nobel Peace Prize and the Empty Chair

On December 10, 2010, an empty chair stood at the Nobel ceremony in Oslo, representing the absent laureate Liu Xiaobo, then imprisoned in Jinzhou, Liaoning. Beijing furiously protested and mobilized other countries to boycott. Yet that empty chair has become one of the most haunting images of 21st-century human rights history.

Death in Custody and the Detention of Liu Xia

In June 2017, Liu was diagnosed with terminal liver cancer; authorities refused his request for overseas treatment. On July 13, 2017, he died at the First Affiliated Hospital of China Medical University in Shenyang. His ashes were forcibly scattered at sea by the authorities and his family was not allowed to keep them. His widow Liu Xia was held under unlawful house arrest for years and was only permitted to leave for medical treatment in Germany in 2018.

Conclusion: His Words Outlive the Prison

Liu Xiaobo was one of the "Four Gentlemen" who urged students to leave Tiananmen Square in 1989 to prevent bloodshed; the petitioner who called for non-violent transition in 2008; and the only Nobel Peace Prize laureate to have died in custody under the People's Republic of China. The authorities believed time would erase him. Yet "I have no enemies" is quietly becoming the moral motto of a new Chinese generation.
